### H2: Is It Worth Learning Czech as an Expat in Prague? (200 words)
- **Answer-first**: Yes — even basic Czech has outsized practical returns, especially for hiring local services
- Cover: Where Czech helps most (dealing with tradespeople, government offices, neighbours, supermarkets), where English is enough (central Prague, tech offices, international restaurants)
- **Key stat**: Only 2–3% of Czechs are fluent in English at a business level (Eurostat language statistics) — so outside Prague's centre, Czech is essential

### H2: Types of Czech Language Learning Available in Prague (250 words)
- **Answer-first**: Private tutor is the fastest for practical results; group class is cheaper; apps fill the gaps
- Cover:
  - Private tutor (best for expats): custom pace, practical focus, 500–800 CZK/hr
  - Group classes (e.g., Czech for Foreigners at language schools): 200–400 CZK/session, less flexible
  - Language apps (Duolingo, Anki, Pimsleur Czech): free/cheap, good for vocabulary but poor for speaking
  - Language exchange (Meetup groups, Tandem app): free, inconsistent quality
- **Chart**: Comparison table — Method × Cost / Speed / Structure / Expat-focus rating

### H2: How to Find a Good Czech Tutor in Prague (300 words)
- **Answer-first**: Use a service marketplace or dedicated tutoring platform — not language school cold calls
- Cover:
  - Platforms: Tool Connect (general service providers including tutors), Preply, iTalki, Superprof
  - What to look for: Experience teaching Czech to English speakers (not just Czech native speakers in general), qualifications (CELTA equivalent, state exam certification), sample lesson available?
  - Red flags: Tutors who use only Czech grammar textbooks without practical focus; tutors who don't ask about your goals upfront
- **Image**: Tutor and student at a café table

### H2: What to Ask a Potential Czech Tutor Before You Book (250 words)
- **Answer-first**: Ask 4 specific questions before committing to a tutor
- Questions:
  1. What's your experience teaching Czech to English-speaking expats specifically?
  2. Do you offer a first trial lesson?
  3. What's your approach — grammar-first or conversation-first?
  4. Can we focus on practical vocabulary for daily life (shopping, dealing with tradespeople, government offices)?
- Cover: Why conversation-first is better for expat purposes, what "trial lesson" should tell you

### H2: Realistic Progress: What 3 Months of Czech Lessons Gets You (250 words)
- **Answer-first**: 3 months of 1 session/week + daily practice = basic survival Czech
- Cover: What you'll be able to do (greet neighbours, handle basic shop interactions, communicate with a tradesperson about what needs fixing), what you won't (hold a complex conversation, read legal documents)
- **Key stat**: Czech is ranked as a Category IV language by the US Foreign Service Institute, requiring 1,100+ hours for full fluency — but survival Czech takes only 50–80 hours
- **Image**: Language learning milestone chart or student making progress

### FAQ Section (4 items)
1. How much does a Czech tutor cost in Prague per hour? — 500–800 CZK/hour for private tutoring; group classes 200–400 CZK/session.
2. How long does it take to learn basic Czech? — 50–80 hours for survival Czech (basic daily interactions); 1,100+ hours for full professional fluency.
3. Is Czech on Duolingo good? — Useful for vocabulary, but Duolingo Czech lacks the speaking focus expats need for daily life. Use it as a supplement, not a standalone method.
4. Can I find a Czech tutor who teaches online? — Yes; most Prague tutors now offer hybrid (in-person + online) sessions.

### Conclusion (100 words)
- Summary: Start with a private tutor who specialises in expat learners; set practical daily-life goals, not fluency goals.
